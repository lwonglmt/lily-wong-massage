Why I practice this way.

I am the founder of Lily Wong Massage Therapy PLLC, a private integrative massage therapy practice. My clinical background includes working in multidisciplinary settings alongside acupuncturists, physical therapists, and chiropractors.

That environment shaped how I approach every client: as a whole person with a specific presentation, not a service to be scheduled. I learned to ask the questions a chiropractor asks — what is the chain of compensation? — and the questions an acupuncturist asks — where is energy held and how is it moving? — and to bring both to my palpation.

I hold a perinatal massage certification and am currently pursuing licensure in acupuncture at Pacific College of Health and Science, deepening the integrative foundation of this practice.

I am a proud member of the American Massage Therapy Association (AMTA), committed to the highest standards of professional practice and continuing education. Detailed session notes are maintained for every client to track progress and support coordinated care with your broader healthcare team.

I don't offer a Swedish-or-deep-tissue menu. I treat the body that walked in. The technique follows.

The Approach

One method shouldn't fit every body.

Integrative massage is a clinical sequence — not a menu. Each session begins with a working diagnosis of what brought you in: a stiff cervical spine after eight hours at a monitor, a hamstring guarding a recent ACL repair, a pelvis adjusting to a third trimester. The hands that follow respond to that finding.

Myofascial release addresses the connective tissue layer the others can't reach. Neuromuscular therapy targets trigger points and nerve compression patterns. Therapeutic deep tissue work addresses deeper layers of muscle and fascia. Therapeutic stretching restores range of motion. Gua sha and cupping decompress and improve circulation. Together, sequenced for your tissue and your hour, they do more than any single modality on its own.

Method

Three principles I won't shortcut, even if you'd let me.

01 / Assessment

Treatment without assessment is a guess.

Every session — first or fiftieth — opens with a focused screen: range of motion, palpation, and a question or two about the week. The minute or two it costs returns the hour as actually-targeted work.

02 / Integration

Modalities serve the body, not the schedule.

Myofascial release, neuromuscular therapy, deep tissue, therapeutic stretching, gua sha, cupping — none of these get an entire hour by default. They get the minutes the tissue needs, in the order it needs them.

03 / Continuity

One session is a treatment. Ten is a plan.

Detailed session notes are maintained for every client. Your second visit reassesses the first. I'll happily refer out — to an acupuncturist, a chiropractor, your PT, to imaging — if the table isn't the right place for the problem.
